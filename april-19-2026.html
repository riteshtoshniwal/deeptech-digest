💰 Funding & Investments

Government Notifies ₹10,000 Crore Startup India Fund of Funds 2.0

The Indian government officially notified the Startup India Fund of Funds 2.0 (FoF 2.0) on April 13, 2026, with a corpus of ₹10,000 crore (~$1.2B USD). Implemented via SIDBI, the scheme takes an indirect approach — investing in Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs), which then deploy capital into DPIIT-recognised startups. Priority sectors include AI, biotechnology, semiconductors, quantum computing, cybersecurity, and advanced manufacturing. The expanded scope explicitly targets deep tech and manufacturing startups, with four distinct segments including a Micro VC segment for early-stage founders.

Southeast Asia Startup Funding Surges 110% YoY to $2.8B in Q1 2026

Southeast Asian startups raised $2.8 billion in Q1 2026, more than doubling the $1.3 billion raised in Q1 2025 — a 110% year-on-year jump. 🔐 Cybersecurity

India Faces 3,195 Cyberattacks Per Week — State-Sponsored Threats Rising

India's cyber threat landscape in 2026 is increasingly shaped by state-sponsored attacks, ransomware, and hacktivism. Indian organisations face an average of 3,195 cyberattacks per week — approximately 702 attacks per minute. The education sector bears the heaviest load (7,684 attacks/org/week), followed by government (4,912) and business services (3,747). Education, healthcare, and manufacturing together account for 47% of malware detections nationally.
- Cyble
- Seqrite Threat Report

Supply Chain Attacks Emerge as Defining Threat Vector in India

One of the most significant shifts in India's 2026 threat landscape is the surge in supply chain attacks, where attackers compromise trusted vendors or service providers to gain downstream access across multiple organisations. This tactic — used by both state-backed threat actors and organised criminal groups — is now a top concern for India's BFSI, defense, and critical infrastructure sectors.

Provisions covering AI-generated content under SGI were formally notified in February 2026 and are already operative; broader social media governance amendments are pending final notification. Global firms operating in India face legal risk under the new framework, particularly around AI training data compliance and deepfake regulation.

India doubled the startup classification window for deep tech companies to 20 years, and raised the revenue threshold for startup-specific tax, grant, and regulatory benefits to ₹3 billion. 📌 Key Takeaways

1. India's AI moment is now in the application layer. After years of model and infrastructure investment globally, India is seizing its opportunity as the world's AI deployment engine — powered by GCCs, a massive developer workforce, and cost-competitive AI startups.

2. Government capital is de-risking Indian deep tech. The ₹10,000 crore FoF 2.0 and the updated 20-year startup classification window signal that India is serious about building long-cycle deep tech companies in AI, semiconductors, biotech, and quantum.

3. Cyber threats in India and SEA are escalating rapidly. State-sponsored attacks, supply chain compromises, and AI-augmented cybercrime are moving faster than defensive frameworks. With 3,000+ weekly attacks on Indian organisations and SEA's policy lag, CISOs in the region face a widening gap.

4. Southeast Asia's tech funding is rebounding sharply — but unevenly. The 110% YoY Q1 2026 surge masks divergent country-level trends. Singapore's explosive growth contrasts with Indonesia's sharp decline — a signal that capital is concentrating in the most mature ecosystems.

5. AI governance is becoming a competitive differentiator. India's IT Rules 2026 and SEA nations' regulatory sandboxes show that the region is moving toward structured AI governance, even as the pace of AI adoption outstrips policy bandwidth. Startups and global firms must plan for compliance complexity ahead.
